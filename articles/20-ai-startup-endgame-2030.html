Dear 2030: Endgame Thinking for AI Founders

~15 min read

In 2024, large models reshaped everyone's expectations of AI. In 2025, agents began transitioning from demos to products. In 2026, what are we living through right now?

If we zoom out to 2030, where will AI be? Which industries will be completely reshaped? And if you're entering the AI space today, what should you be building toward?

This isn't a prediction piece — predicting the future is nearly impossible. This is a thinking framework to help you find certainty amid uncertainty.

Part 1: Where AI Might Be by 2030

1. Model Capability: From "Smart Tool" to "Reliable Partner"

Where we are in 2026: Large models are smart but unreliable. They can write beautiful copy, but they'll confidently hallucinate. They can write code, but humans need to review it. They can do many things, but you can't fully trust them.

Where we might be by 2030:

  • Massively improved reliability: Through reinforcement learning, human feedback, and formal verification, AI reliability might jump from "90% correct" to "99.9% correct." That gap sounds small, but it means AI can shift from "assistant" to "autonomous executor."
  • Deep multimodal fusion: Text, images, video, audio, 3D, code — the barriers between these modalities will dissolve. AI will understand all types of information simultaneously and convert freely between them.
  • World models emerge: AI will understand not just language, but the physical laws governing reality. This is the foundation for embodied intelligence (robots).

2. Agents: From "Usable" to "Genuinely Useful"

Where we are in 2026: AI agents can handle some simple task chains — searching, writing docs, sending emails. But complex tasks still break down and require human intervention.

Where we might be by 2030:

  • Autonomous work capability: Agents can independently complete complex workflows — from receiving a task to delivering results — without human intervention in between.
  • Collaboration: Multiple agents can work together on tasks, dividing labor like a team.
  • Memory and learning: Agents can learn from experience, remember user preferences and habits, getting better with use.
  • Specialized roles: Different agents for different domains — coding agents, design agents, marketing agents, legal agents — forming an "AI team."

3. Embodied Intelligence: AI Enters the Physical World

Where we are in 2026: Robots can walk and grasp objects, but they're far from "useful." Self-driving works in specific scenarios, but full autonomy is still distant.

Where we might be by 2030:

  • Home robots: Robots that can do housework and help care for the elderly and children may start entering homes. Not sci-fi level, but capable of basic tasks.
  • Industrial robot upgrade: From fixed, repetitive welding/assembly to flexible, general-purpose workers that can handle exceptions.
  • Autonomous driving goes mainstream: L4 autonomy may be fully deployed in specific zones (urban arterials, highways).

4. AI-Native Apps: From "AI-Enhanced" to "AI-First"

Where we are in 2026: Most AI applications are "traditional product + AI features" — layering AI capabilities onto existing products.

Where we might be by 2030:

  • Entirely new categories emerge: Just as the iPhone created the smartphone category, AI-native product forms will appear. These products are designed for AI from day one, not bolted onto old ones.
  • Interaction paradigm shift: Voice, gesture, brain-computer interfaces... keyboards and mice may no longer be the primary input methods.
  • Extreme personalization: Everyone's AI assistant is unique — it knows everything about you and customizes everything for you.

Part 2: Which Industries Will Be Completely Reshaped?

1. Education: From Standardized to Personalized

Today: One teacher faces 40 students with uniform textbooks, uniform pacing, uniform exams.

By 2030:

  • Every student has an AI tutor that understands their learning style, knowledge gaps, and interests.
  • Learning paths are fully personalized — not everyone follows the same sequence.
  • Exams may be replaced by "continuous assessment" — AI evaluates your abilities in real time, no standardized test needed.
  • The teacher's role shifts from "knowledge transmitter" to "learning guide" and "emotional support."

Startup opportunities: AI-native education platforms, personalized learning engines, AI teaching assistants.

2. Healthcare: From "Treating Disease" to "Preventing Disease"

Today: You get sick, see a doctor, and the doctor diagnoses based on experience and test results.

By 2030:

  • AI continuously monitors your health data, issuing warnings before you get sick.
  • AI-assisted diagnosis accuracy exceeds most doctors (especially in imaging).
  • Drug development cycles shrink from 10 years to 2–3 years.
  • Telemedicine + AI becomes the norm, alleviating healthcare resource imbalances.

Startup opportunities: Health monitoring AI, AI diagnostic platforms, AI drug discovery tools.

3. Finance: From "Human Decisions" to "AI Decisions"

Today: Risk control relies on rules + manual review. Investment relies on experience + research.

By 2030:

  • Risk control is fully handled by AI in real time; humans only deal with exceptions.
  • Personal finance is entirely managed by AI, automatically adjusting based on your risk tolerance and life goals.
  • Small loan approvals may be fully automated.
  • Financial regulation also uses AI — real-time market anomaly monitoring.

Startup opportunities: AI risk control, robo-advisors, AI compliance tools.

4. Content Creation: From "Human-Made" to "Human-AI Collaboration"

Today: AI can generate text, images, and video, but quality can't fully replace humans.

By 2030:

  • 80% of commercial content (ads, marketing copy, product descriptions) is AI-generated.
  • Movie and game production costs drop dramatically — AI handles most repetitive work.
  • Personalized content becomes the norm — the news, stories, and ads each person sees are tailored for them.
  • The definition of "creator" changes — from "making it by hand" to "directing AI to make it."

Startup opportunities: AI content creation tools, personalized content platforms, AI copyright management.

5. Software Development: From "Writing Code" to "Describing Requirements"

Today: Programmers write code; AI assists with completion.

By 2030:

  • Most routine code is AI-generated; the programmer's role becomes "architect + reviewer."
  • Non-technical people can "develop" simple apps — describing requirements in natural language, with AI generating the code.
  • Software development speed increases 10x or more.
  • The definition of "programming" changes — from "writing code" to "describing problems and constraints."

Startup opportunities: AI development platforms, low-code + AI tools, AI testing and DevOps.

Part 3: Endgame Thinking for AI Startups

1. AI Is Not the Goal — It's the Means

This is the most important point. AI itself is not the product; solving user problems is the product.

The mistake many AI founders make: "I have this AI capability — now let me find a scenario to use it." The correct approach: "This scenario has this problem — can AI solve it better?"

By 2030, the label "AI company" may be meaningless — just as nobody today says "I'm an internet company." AI will become infrastructure, like electricity and water. What matters is what problem you solve with it.

2. Data Is the New Moat

Models will become increasingly commoditized. Open source will catch up with closed source. But data is unique.

By 2030, an AI company's core competitive advantage may not be how good its model is, but:

  • What data do you have that nobody else does?
  • How strong are your data's network effects? (More users → better data → better product → more users)
  • How high are your data barriers?

3. Trust Is the Greatest Asset

As AI makes more and more decisions on your behalf, your trust in AI becomes critical.

By 2030, users may choose AI products not based on "who's smarter" but on "who's more trustworthy." This includes:

  • Data privacy protection
  • Decision explainability
  • Accountability when things go wrong
  • Alignment of values

4. Vertical Beats Horizontal

Competition in general-purpose large models is already fierce, and only a handful of winners will emerge.

But the opportunity in verticals is enormous:

  • Every industry has its own specialized needs, data formats, and compliance requirements.
  • Vertical AI can outperform general-purpose AI by a wide margin.
  • Vertical customers have stronger willingness to pay.

Advice: If you're entering AI today, prioritize verticals.

5. Going Global Is Mandatory

AI is inherently global technology — models don't need to be retrained for each country.

By 2030, AI startups may be global from day one:

  • Products serving global users
  • Teams distributed worldwide
  • Data sourced from global markets

Chinese founders' edge: Chinese AI products offer excellent cost-performance, strong engineering capabilities, and fast iteration speeds. These advantages are highly competitive in global markets.

Part 4: Advice for Everyone Entering AI

If You're a Technical Person

  • Don't just learn models — learn products. Technology is a means; product is the end. Understanding user needs matters more than understanding transformers.
  • Focus on engineering skills. The ability to deploy models to production is rarer than the ability to train them.
  • Keep learning. The pace of AI change is unprecedented. Spend at least 10 hours a week learning new things.

If You're a Founder

  • Validate before investing. Don't build a big team on day one. Use the smallest team to validate core assumptions.
  • Find real pain points. Don't use AI for AI's sake. First find what users actually need to solve.
  • Take the business model seriously. No matter how good the tech, if it doesn't make money, it's a dead end. Figure out monetization early.
  • Stay flexible. AI moves too fast. You'll need to pivot frequently. Keep the team small and agile.

If You're an Investor

  • Team matters more than tech. AI startup success isn't about how advanced the technology is — it's whether the team can adapt and find the market.
  • Business model matters more than model capability. Model capabilities get commoditized; business model moats are more durable.
  • Be patient. AI startup cycles are longer than internet startup cycles. Don't expect a 3-year exit.

If You're a Traditional Industry Professional

  • Embrace AI; don't resist it. AI won't replace your industry, but people who use AI will replace people who don't.
  • Understand AI's capability boundaries. Know what AI can and can't do — that's where real application opportunities lie.
  • Partner with AI founders. You have domain knowledge; they have technical capability. Collaboration beats going solo.

Part 5: The 2030 AI Startup Landscape

Let me paint a bold picture of what AI entrepreneurship might look like by 2030:

  • Foundation layer: A handful of large companies (OpenAI, Google, Meta, and a few in China) provide general-purpose large models. Competition at this level is essentially over.
  • Infrastructure layer: A multitude of companies provide AI infrastructure — data labeling, model fine-tuning, deployment tools, safety auditing. The opportunity here is "selling picks and shovels."
  • Application layer: The biggest opportunity. Every industry has AI-native startups redefining how the industry operates.
  • Device layer: AI hardware — robots, smart glasses, in-car AI — begins entering daily life.

The biggest winners may not be companies building models, but companies using models to solve real problems.

Just as the biggest winners of the internet era weren't the companies building routers, but the companies using the internet to transform shopping (Amazon), social (Facebook), and search (Google).

Conclusion

AI entrepreneurship is not a sprint — it's a marathon. Technology changes, markets shift, competitive landscapes evolve. But some things remain constant:

  • Users have needs that must be met.
  • Businesses need sustainable models.
  • Founders need resilient will.

If you're entering the AI space today, don't let short-term hype and hot money cloud your thinking. Figure out what problem you're solving, what moat you're building, and what path you're walking.

Then, keep walking.

The AI world of 2030 belongs to those who make the right choices today.
