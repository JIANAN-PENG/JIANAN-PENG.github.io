Three Waves of AI: From Hand-Crafted Rules to Large-Scale Models

~3,800 words · 12 min read

Prologue: Why Bother Learning AI History?

Many people think AI was born when ChatGPT dropped in 2022. Not even close. Artificial intelligence as a discipline is nearly 70 years old. The ChatGPTs, Midjourneys, and Copilots of today didn't appear out of thin air — they're the culmination of decades of accumulated progress.

Understanding history isn't about archaeology. It's about grasping why AI ended up where it is today, and where it's headed next. Once you understand the rhythm of these waves, you can tell where you're standing and where the opportunities lie.

In this article, we'll walk through the three most important waves in AI history.

The First Wave: Expert Systems & Symbolism (1956 – Early 1990s)

The Starting Point: The Dartmouth Conference

In the summer of 1956, a group of young scientists gathered at Dartmouth College for a two-month workshop. The organizer was John McCarthy — the man who coined the term "Artificial Intelligence." Attendees included Marvin Minsky, Claude Shannon, Allen Newell, Herbert Simon, and other future godfathers of AI.

The core hypothesis of that meeting was bold: human intelligence can be described using symbols and rules. Translate an expert's knowledge into rules a computer can follow, and the machine will think and decide just like that expert.

This approach later became known as Symbolism.

The Golden Age of Expert Systems

During the 1970s–80s, the symbolic approach hit its stride. The flagship product was the Expert System.

What's an expert system? Simply put, it takes domain expertise and encodes it as a stack of "if… then…" rules inside a computer.

Famous examples include:

  • MYCIN (1970s): A medical diagnostic system for blood infections. It contained roughly 600 rules and reportedly achieved 69% diagnostic accuracy — outperforming some human doctors.
  • XCON/R1 (1980s): DEC's system for configuring computer orders, saving the company tens of millions of dollars annually.
  • Prolog: A programming language purpose-built for AI. Japan even made it the centerpiece of its "Fifth Generation Computer" project.

In that era, entire nations bet big on AI. Japan's government poured $850 million into its Fifth Generation project; the UK and US launched similar national programs. Venture capital and corporations flooded in.

The Bubble Bursts: The First AI Winter

But problems surfaced quickly.

First, the knowledge acquisition bottleneck. Extracting an expert's knowledge rule by rule was painfully slow and expensive. Building a single expert system could take years.

Second, brittleness. Expert systems performed well within their narrow domain, but step slightly outside and they'd produce absurd answers. They had no "common sense."

Third, insufficient compute. The hardware of the time simply couldn't support complex reasoning or large-scale data processing.

By the late 1980s, Japan's Fifth Generation project had fallen short of its goals. Investment dried up, governments slashed funding, and by the early 1990s the first AI winter had arrived.

Impact on Everyday People

Frankly, the first wave barely touched ordinary people. Expert systems were mostly used inside enterprises, and the public had almost no exposure. But the wave planted a critical seed: machines can outperform human experts at specific tasks — a seed that would eventually bloom into something extraordinary.

The Second Wave: The Rise of Machine Learning (1990s – Early 2010s)

A Shift in Philosophy: From "Teaching" to "Learning"

The first wave was about humans teaching machines — write the rules, and the machine follows them. The second wave took a fundamentally different approach: let machines learn from data on their own.

How did this shift happen? People realized that for many tasks, you simply can't write rules. How do you write rules to detect whether a photo contains a cat? How do you write rules to classify spam? The rules would be too numerous, too complex, too easy to circumvent — the rule-based approach to describing the world hit a dead end.

The solution? Let the machine look at the data and find patterns itself.

Key Breakthroughs

  • Support Vector Machines (SVM): Introduced by Vladimir Vapnik in the 1990s, SVMs delivered outstanding classification on small datasets and became the era's "killer algorithm."
  • Random Forests & Ensemble Learning: Combine multiple "weak learners" into one "strong learner" — simple idea, exceptional results.
  • Bayesian Methods: The backbone of spam filtering. Paul Graham's 2002 essay A Plan for Spam used naive Bayes to largely solve the spam problem, cleaning up inboxes worldwide.
  • The Data Explosion from the Internet: The rise of Google, Amazon, and Facebook generated oceans of data. Machine learning algorithms finally had enough fuel.
  • Kaggle Competitions (founded 2010): Turned data science into a competitive sport, massively accelerating algorithm iteration and adoption.

Key Figures & Companies

  • Andrew Ng: Stanford professor, later head of Google Brain and Baidu AI, co-founder of Coursera. His machine learning course introduced millions worldwide to the field.
  • Geoffrey Hinton: A pioneer of deep learning (more on him in the third wave), though his early work began during this era.
  • Google: Born as a search engine, it was fundamentally a machine learning company. PageRank is a classic ML application.
  • IBM Watson: Defeated human champions on Jeopardy! in 2011, becoming AI's breakout mainstream moment.

Impact on Everyday People

This time, ordinary people finally felt AI's presence:

  • Search got smarter: Google stopped being just keyword matching and started understanding intent.
  • Spam filtering: Your inbox suddenly got clean.
  • Recommendation systems: Netflix recommended movies, Amazon recommended products, YouTube kept you scrolling.
  • Voice assistants: Siri (2011), Google Now (2012) appeared. Clunky, sure — but you could talk to your phone.

One-sentence summary of the second wave: AI walked out of the lab and into your pocket.

The Third Wave: Deep Learning & Large Models (2012 – Present)

The ImageNet Moment

In 2012, a neural network called AlexNet crushed the ImageNet image recognition competition — its error rate was more than 10 percentage points lower than the runner-up.

This wasn't just winning a contest. It proved something fundamental: when you have enough data and enough compute, deep neural networks vastly outperform traditional machine learning.

AlexNet was created by Alex Krizhevsky, Ilya Sutskever, and Geoffrey Hinton. Ilya later co-founded OpenAI; Hinton won the 2024 Nobel Prize in Physics.

This moment is often called "AI's iPhone moment."

Why Did Deep Learning Suddenly Explode?

Three conditions matured simultaneously:

  1. Big Data: The internet had accumulated massive labeled datasets (ImageNet alone had 14 million labeled images).
  2. Big Compute: NVIDIA GPUs turned out to be perfect for neural networks. A single gaming GPU outperformed an entire rack of CPUs.
  3. Algorithmic Breakthroughs: ReLU activations, Dropout regularization, Batch Normalization, residual connections (ResNet) — a cascade of techniques finally made deep networks trainable.

All three were essential. Deep learning didn't fail before because the ideas were wrong — it failed because data and compute hadn't caught up.

From Narrow to General: The Large Model Era

In 2017, Google published a world-changing paper: "Attention Is All You Need," introducing the Transformer architecture.

Transformers were originally designed for machine translation, but people quickly discovered the architecture was nearly universal — it could handle text, images, audio, video, almost any type of data.

The rest of the story you probably know:

  • 2018: Google released BERT; NLP was permanently disrupted.
  • 2020: OpenAI released GPT-3 with 175 billion parameters — the first time ordinary people felt the shock of "AI can write."
  • November 2022: ChatGPT launched. 100 million users in two months. AI went from tech-circle topic to household conversation.
  • 2023–2024: GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, Llama, Mistral — large models bloomed everywhere. Multimodal capabilities exploded — AI could read, write, see, hear, and generate video.
  • 2024–2025: Reasoning models (o1, DeepSeek-R1) emerged; AI began "thinking" instead of just "recalling." Agent concepts took off, and AI started autonomously completing complex tasks.

Key Figures

  • Ilya Sutskever: OpenAI co-founder who drove the GPT series. Later founded Safe Superintelligence Inc.
  • Sam Altman: OpenAI CEO who pushed AI from the lab to the mass market.
  • Demis Hassabis: DeepMind founder, the mind behind AlphaGo and AlphaFold.
  • Jensen Huang: NVIDIA CEO, the "arms dealer" of AI compute. Without his GPUs, this wave might not have started.
  • Liang Wenfeng: DeepSeek founder, who trained GPT-4-class models at a fraction of the cost, shaking the entire industry.
  • Yang Zhilin: Moonshot AI founder and Kimi creator, representing China's new generation of LLM startups.

Impact on Everyday People

This wave's impact is revolutionary and all-encompassing:

  • Writing & Creation: AI writes emails, reports, code, paints, composes music, and makes videos. The barrier to content creation has plummeted.
  • Work Productivity: Copilot writes your code, AI builds your decks, auto-summaries read your papers. Knowledge workers are several times more productive.
  • Education: AI can be everyone's private tutor, explaining any concept in terms you understand.
  • Healthcare: AlphaFold solved protein folding; AI-assisted diagnosis now surpasses human doctors in many fields.
  • Autonomous Driving: Tesla, Waymo, Baidu Apollo — not fully realized yet, but already reshaping how we get around.

Patterns Across the Three Waves

1. Waves Are Separated by "Winters"

After every hype cycle, overblown expectations and immature tech trigger a trough. The first winter hit in the early 1990s; the second (sometimes called the "nuclear winter") came in the mid-2000s. We're now at the peak of the third wave — how long will it last? Will there be another winter? Every practitioner is thinking about this.

2. Each Wave's Core Breakthrough Is a New Way of Learning

  • First wave: Humans write rules (Symbolism)
  • Second wave: Machines learn from data (Machine Learning)
  • Third wave: Machines learn general capabilities from massive data (Deep Learning + Large Models)

At its core, it's about how humans teach machines evolving — from hand-holding, to giving them textbooks to self-study, to handing them an entire library and letting them figure it out.

3. Each Wave Expands the Blast Radius

  • First wave: Enterprise-internal; ordinary people felt nothing.
  • Second wave: Entered consumer internet; people started noticing.
  • Third wave: Penetrates daily life, fundamentally transforming work and creation.

4. Infrastructure Maturity Is the Hidden Catalyst

The first wave was constrained by hardware, the second benefited from the internet and data, and the third is powered by GPUs and large-scale distributed training. Technology waves don't arrive because of a single genius's flash of insight — they arrive because infrastructure accumulates to a tipping point.

Where Do We Stand Now?

If the first wave was "AI learned rules," and the second was "AI learned patterns," then the essence of the third wave is: AI learned a universal representation of language and knowledge.

We're far from done. Large model capabilities are still improving rapidly, and new directions — Agents, world models, embodied intelligence — are just sprouting.

Understanding history isn't about predicting the future — no one can do that accurately. It's about being able to distinguish real trends from hype when the next wave hits.

After all, AI's history tells us: true technological revolutions never happen overnight. They erupt after countless failures, winters, and sheer persistence.
