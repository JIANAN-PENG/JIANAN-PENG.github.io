Moats Reimagined: How AI Companies Build Competitive Advantages

~12 min read

A Brutal Reality: Your AI Product Can Be Cloned in 3 Months

In early 2024, an AI writing tool launched with impressive copy generation capabilities and quickly went viral, surpassing one million monthly active users. Three months later, a dozen near-identical competitors appeared. Some were even better and cheaper.

The founder asked me: "Where's my moat?"

I went silent. Because honestly, if your moat is just "our model is good" or "we shipped first," then you don't really have one.

This is the harshest reality of AI entrepreneurship: In an era of converging model capabilities and thriving open-source ecosystems, traditional competitive barriers are dissolving — but new ones haven't fully formed yet.

This article explores how AI companies should actually build moats.

Do Traditional Internet Moats Still Work?

Let's review the classic moats from the traditional internet era:

Network Effects

The more users, the more valuable the product. WeChat is the classic example — all your friends are on WeChat; you can't switch to another messaging app.

Does it apply in AI? Partially.

For AI social products (Character.ai) and AI collaboration tools (Notion AI), network effects still hold. But for most AI tooling products (AI writing, AI art, AI coding), network effects are weak — you use your AI writing tool, I use mine, and neither affects the other.

Data Flywheel

More users → more data → better model → better product → more users. Google Search works this way.

Does it apply in AI? Theoretically yes, but practically challenging.

The catch: general-purpose LLMs are trained on public internet data, not your user data. Your usage data does have value, but how much model advantage it translates into depends on many factors.

Economies of Scale

Greater scale → lower unit costs. AWS is the classic example.

Does it apply in AI? Yes, but differently.

AI's scale effects mainly manifest in inference costs — more users help amortize the fixed costs of GPU clusters. But this is fundamentally different from traditional internet scale effects (where marginal cost approaches zero). Every AI inference has real computational cost.

Brand Recognition

When users think of a category, they think of you first. Search → Google. E-commerce → Taobao.

Does it apply in AI? Yes, but brand formation is accelerating — and so is brand erosion.

ChatGPT became synonymous with "AI chat" almost overnight. But how long can that brand advantage last? If Google launches a clearly superior AI product tomorrow, the switching cost for users isn't high.

Summary: Traditional moats haven't completely disappeared, but each faces new challenges in the AI era.

Where Are the New Moats for AI Companies?

Since traditional moats aren't enough, where should AI companies look for new barriers? I've identified four directions.

Moat #1: Proprietary Data — What Data Do You Have That Nobody Else Does?

This is the most overrated — and most underrated — moat in the AI era.

Why overrated? Because many people assume "data equals moat." But data's value depends on:

  1. Data uniqueness. If others can access the same data, it's not a moat.
  2. Data quality. Garbage data doesn't help — it hurts the model.
  3. Data-to-product conversion ability. Having data isn't the same as using it well.

Why underrated? Because truly valuable data moats aren't about "volume" — they're about "irreplaceability."

What makes data irreplaceable?

  • User behavior data. How users interact with your product — what they click, what they abandon, what they modify. This reflects genuine user preferences that competitors can't access.
  • Industry-proprietary data. Medical imaging, legal case law, financial trading data, industrial sensor data. These have high acquisition barriers.
  • Feedback loop data. User evaluations, corrections, and annotations of AI outputs. This directly tells the model "what's right and what's wrong."

Case: Tesla's data moat.

Tesla has millions of cars on the road, collecting massive driving data daily — road conditions, driving behavior, edge cases. This data is something Waymo and Baidu Apollo cannot replicate in the short term. Tesla's autonomous driving system is built on this data flywheel.

How to build this moat:

  1. Start collecting user interaction data as early as possible, even with few users.
  2. Design a data collection loop: users use product → generates data → data improves model → product gets better → more usage.
  3. Mind data compliance. GDPR and privacy laws are tightening; data collection must be legal and compliant.

Moat #2: Engineering Excellence — The "Last Mile" from Model to Product

Many people only see model-level competition, overlooking this: between model and product lies a long engineering journey.

What does engineering excellence look like?

  • Prompt engineering. The same model can produce wildly different results with different prompts. Accumulated prompt libraries and tuning experience are barriers.
  • RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) architecture. How to efficiently retrieve relevant knowledge, organize context, and manage multi-turn conversation context — these all have high technical barriers.
  • Evaluation systems. How to measure AI output quality? How to automate testing? How to continuously monitor model performance? These capabilities don't appear in papers but are critical for products.
  • Cost optimization. How to reduce inference costs while maintaining quality? Model distillation, quantization, caching strategies, request routing — all engineering capabilities.
  • Reliability assurance. AI products are more prone to surprises than traditional software — models can hallucinate, output harmful content, or crash on edge cases. Building guardrails, fallback mechanisms, and human review processes — all engineering capabilities.

Case: Cursor's engineering moat.

Cursor is an AI coding assistant built on Claude and GPT APIs. It doesn't have its own model, but its product experience far surpasses using the APIs directly. Why?

Because it invested heavily in engineering:

  • Carefully designed context management that lets AI understand entire codebases
  • Intelligent code completion strategies that go beyond simple continuation
  • Deep IDE integration for a seamless user experience
  • Fast response times through caching and pre-computation optimization

These engineering capabilities can't be replicated by cloning a UI.

How to build this moat:

  1. Invest in engineering teams, not just algorithm teams.
  2. Build systematic prompt management and optimization processes.
  3. Create automated evaluation systems for continuous monitoring and improvement.
  4. Accumulate industry-specific engineering experience — solutions differ vastly across scenarios.

Moat #3: User Habits & Workflow Embedding — Make Users Can't Live Without You

This is the most underrated moat.

When your product is deeply embedded in users' daily workflows, switching costs become enormous. This cost isn't just "switching tools" — it's:

  • Data migration cost. Data, templates, and configurations accumulated in your product are painful to migrate.
  • Learning cost. Users already know your product; switching means learning from scratch.
  • Habit cost. Humans are creatures of habit. Once a routine forms, the motivation to change is weak.
  • Integration cost. If your product deeply integrates with other tools (Slack, Jira, Salesforce, etc.), switching means rebuilding the entire workflow.

Case: Notion AI's embedding strategy.

Notion isn't an AI company, but it seamlessly embedded AI into the note-taking and document workflow. When users write documents, AI is right there, always available. Over time, AI becomes part of the writing process — users never think about switching to a "better AI writing tool" because that tool isn't in their workflow.

How to build this moat:

  1. Integrate into users' existing workflows as early as possible, rather than asking users to change their habits for you.
  2. When designing import/export features, follow the "easy to export, even easier to import" strategy.
  3. Build a rich integration ecosystem — the more connections to mainstream tools, the harder it is to leave.
  4. Offer personalization — let users customize the product to their needs. More customization = higher switching costs.

Moat #4: Brand Trust — "I Trust You Not to Screw Up"

In the AI era, brand trust matters more than ever. Why?

Because AI products have a unique problem: users don't know when AI will make a mistake.

With traditional software, functionality is deterministic — click this button, get that result. AI products are different; the same input can produce different outputs, and sometimes they inexplicably fail.

Under this uncertainty, users choose AI products based on: which brand do I trust?

Where does brand trust come from?

  • Track record of reliability. Your product has consistently worked well with few serious issues.
  • Transparency. You're willing to tell users where AI's capability boundaries are, without overpromising.
  • Security. User data is safe with you; you don't misuse it.
  • Values. Your company has clear values that users identify with.

Case: Anthropic's trust-brand strategy.

Anthropic made "safety" its core brand proposition from day one. Its product is named Claude (not "SuperAI"). Its messaging centers on "helpful, harmless, and honest." In an industry rife with hype and safety incidents, Anthropic's "safety" brand image became one of its core competitive advantages.

Many enterprise clients choose Claude not because it scores highest on benchmarks, but because "Anthropic seems more trustworthy."

How to build this moat:

  1. Prioritize product stability over feature richness. A stable, reliable 10-point product beats an inconsistent 15-point product.
  2. Be honest about AI's capability boundaries. Telling users "AI might not be great at this task" is better than pretending it can do everything.
  3. Build safety incident response mechanisms. Responding quickly and transparently when things go wrong actually builds trust.
  4. Invest in content marketing and community building. Help users understand your technical philosophy and values.

Moat Combination Strategy

A single moat is fragile. The best AI companies combine multiple moats.

Examples:

  • Data + Engineering: Tesla (proprietary driving data + end-to-end engineering capability)
  • Engineering + User Habits: Cursor (deep engineering optimization + IDE workflow embedding)
  • Brand Trust + User Habits: Notion AI (brand trust + document workflow embedding)
  • Data + Brand Trust: Anthropic (safe data practices + safety brand image)

The more moats, the deeper the competitive barrier.

A Timeline Perspective: How Moats Are Built

Moats aren't built in a day. Different stages call for different focus areas:

Early Stage (0 → 1)

Focus: Product experience + rapid iteration

The most important thing early on is making users feel "this product is great." Data flywheels and brand trust come later. Survive first.

This stage's moat is primarily speed — understanding user needs faster, iterating faster, fixing issues faster than competitors.

Growth Stage (1 → 10)

Focus: Data accumulation + engineering capability

User numbers are growing; you need to systematically accumulate data and optimize engineering. Start building evaluation systems, data collection loops, and cost optimization plans.

Maturity Stage (10 → 100)

Focus: User habits + brand trust

The product is stable, user base is large enough. This stage focuses on deepening workflow embedding, building brand awareness and trust, while considering industry expansion and ecosystem development.

Some Counterintuitive Insights

Insight #1: Open Source Isn't the Enemy of Moats — It's a Weapon

Many think that as open-source models proliferate, AI moats weaken. But flip it around: if you can build strong engineering capabilities and data flywheels around open-source models, open source becomes your advantage — you don't need to spend fortunes training base models, freeing you to focus on higher-value work.

Insight #2: Speed Is a Moat, But Not a Long-Term One

"We're faster than competitors!" — This is indeed an advantage early on. But speed is replicable. If your only moat is "fast," you're in trouble once competitors speed up too.

Speed is a means, not an end. The first-mover advantage gained through speed should be converted into other moats as quickly as possible.

Insight #3: The Best Moat Is Making Users Feel "No Reason to Switch"

Users don't leave your product not because they "can't" (lock-in), but because they "don't need to" (your product is good enough).

Lock-in is fragile — once a better option appears, users leave without hesitation. But "no reason to switch" is powerful — as long as your product stays great, users stay.

Conclusion

In the AI era, moats are shifting from "what I have" (assets) to "what I can continuously do" (capabilities).

Traditional moat thinking: I own a mine that nobody else has, so I have a barrier.
AI-era moat thinking: I have a system for continuously mining — and it gets faster and better the more I mine.

Data, engineering, user habits, brand trust — these four pillars hold up an AI company's competitive barriers.

But ultimately, all moats share the same foundation: continuously creating value for users. When users find your product useful, trustworthy, and indispensable — that's the deepest moat of all.
